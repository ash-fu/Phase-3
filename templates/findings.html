Leeds


Leeds is a city in West Yorkshire, England; like most cities in England they have a sophisticated road update system. They have continuous updates on incidents and roadworks, indicating severity of incident and location, including nearby roads affected. Traffic camera footage is also available. You can find more information and updates on road incidents at BBC.






Introduction to Data Analysis


Data

The following observations on Leeds Road Accidents in 2012 are based on data provided by the Leeds City Council .
Moreover, the additional information are obtained from these 3 reports: Great Britain Main Results 2012 , Great Britain Annual Report 2012 , West Yorkshire Reported Casualties 2014 .

Limitation of Data

Most of the statistics are based on road accidents reported to the police (STATS19 system). However, a limitation of this dataset, is that a considerable proportion of non-fatal casualties are not reported to the police; since data from hospital, compensation claims and surveys indicate a higher number of casualties than reported. (gov.uk)

Definition of Accident

In these observations an accident had been defined as:
Involving personal injury occurring on the public highway (including footways) in which at least one road vehicle or a vehicle in collision with a pedestrian is involved. And becomes known to the police within 30 days of its accident. Damage-only accidents, with no human casualties or accidents on private roads or car parks are not included.

Reported Road Casualties in Great Britain: Great Britain Annual Report 2012


Since the start of the 2007 recession, young people (especially young males) are on average driving less. Hence, this may be influencing the falls in fatalities (and casualties of all severities) within this age group.


Despite this, the number of young drivers in vehicle accidents are still high as:

  • A fifth of people killed or seriously injured in a reported road accident in Great Britain during 2012 were involved in a collision where at least one of the cars was driven by a young driver
  • Around 22 per cent of all accidents involved at least one young driver.
  • The 32,400 accidents involving at least one young car driver resulted in 350 deaths and more than 4,100 seriously injured casualties.
  • Nearly a quarter of all car drivers (133 out of 542 drivers) who died in 2012 were young drivers themselves.
  • Young car drivers are involved in accidents are considerably higher than would be expected given the distance they drive.


Reported Road Casualties in West Yorkshire: Main Results in 2014


The number of young car drivers and passengers aged 16 to 29 had been falling steadily, and fell substantially in 2010 and further in 2011 before rising in 2012, 2013.

Additionaly, for young children, boys are twice as likely as girls to suffer fatal or serious injury.

Also, the downward trend since 2005 in killed or seriously injured casualties amongst the more mature age group of 30 to 59 has slowed down considerably, where there was a substantial fall in 2011 and 2012 but then an increase in 2013.

Reported Road Casualties in Great Britain: Great Britain Annual Report 2012


Car occupants make up the largest group of road accident casualties across all severities (36 per cent of serious and 65 per cent of slight). Also, there has been a decreasing trend in car accidents; one hypothesis for the rapid decrease over this period is the economic downturn and recession from 2007 and heavy snowfall in 2010.

However, the vulnerable road user groups, and especially motorcyclists and pedal cyclists make up considerably larger proportions of the seriously injured casualties. The three vulnerable road user groups (pedestrians, pedal cyclists and motorcyclists) between them account for almost 50 per cent of all deaths and 60 per cent of all seriously injured casualties. Also it is the eighth year that the number of seriously injured cyclist casualties has increased.

Also eventhough, vulnerable road users only account for around 29 per cent of slightly injured casualties. This might not be indicative of anything to do with accidents and casualties, but rather an artefact of underreporting. Pedal cyclists and pedestrians, especially slightly injured casualties, are both road user groups who are more likely to be underreported in the road accident data collected by the police. Therefore the data held in the database might under- represent these groups.


The relative risk for vulnerable road users is still high as:

  • Motorcycle users, per mile ridden, are roughly 35 times more likely to be killed in a road traffic accident than car occupants.
  • Pedestrians and pedal cyclists, per mile walked and cycled respectively, are roughly 11 times more likely to be killed in a road accident than car occupants.
  • Motorcyclists are over 50 times and pedal cyclists 30 times more likely to be seriously injured in a reported road accident than a car occupant.


Reported Road Casualties in West Yorkshire: Main Results in 2014


While the car is the largest contributor to vehicle accidents there has been a downward trend in the number of car occupants killed or seriously injured. It has effectively levelled off between 2005 and 2008 before falling for the following 5 years between 2010 and 2013.

In comparison the number of cyclists injured on the county’s roads has increased consistently since 2001. In the recent five years, the increase is even higher: 39% more collisions between 2010 and 2014. The number of adult cyclist casualties has almost doubled since 2001. This recent surge in cycling popularity may explain the persistent increase in cyclist casualties.

It is also noteworthy, that Leeds district recorded the highest number (340) of cyclist casualties. And the large majority of these collisions occurred during commuting times of weekdays, in daylight, and on dry road surfaces which links to the Weather Observation further on in this page.

Hypothesis

Poor weather conditions, lighting conditions, road surface causes road accidents.

Observation

From the data in 2012 in Leeds, fine weather, daylight and dry roads are the largest contributors to vehicle accidents in Leeds.
A reasonable assumption is that fine weather, daylight and dry roads are not the reason for vehicle accidents.

Reported Road Casualties in Great Britain: Main Results in 2012


In 2012 it was the second wettest year on record, behind 2000. Eight of the last nine months of the year had considerably more rainfall than the 1981-2010 long term average. In particular, there were sustained periods of heavy rainfall during late spring and early summer in the UK. Rainfall in April was 79 per cent higher than the April long term average. More than double the average June rainfall fell in June 2012. The equivalent figure for July was 53 per cent higher than the long term average for that month. The second quarter of 2012 (April to June) was the wettest second quarter since Met Office records began in 1910.

The likely result of this additional rainfall in 2012 would have been to reduce the number of pedestrians, pedal cyclists and motorcyclists on Britain’s roads, especially during the spring and summer months. A reduction in exposure in this way may have had the effect of reducing the number of accidents and casualties from these user groups. Other road users, such as car occupants, are less likely to have been affected by the heavy rainfall.

Therefore it is reasonable to assume that the unusually heavy rainfall suppressed the number of vulnerable users on the roads and footways, particularly at a time when they could have been expected to be most active. This may have lead to a lower number of casualties than might have been expected during that period.



Reported Road Casualties in Great Britain: Great Britain Annual Report 2012


The return to the long term downward trend in fatalities in 2012 after the increase between 2010 and 2011 reinforces the hypothesis that the 2010 figures were affected by the weather.

In 2010 there were two periods of significant and extended snowfall throughout Britain. The net effect of the snow was probably to reduce the number of reported road casualties that year. The year 2010 still remains the year with the record low number of serious injuries and killed or serious injury casualties. Secondly to this, 2012 was an unusually wet year. 2012 was the second wettest year on record and the second quarter, during the months of April, May and June, was the wettest quarter 2 on record. Again, the effect of the heavy rainfall, especially as it fell during late spring and most of summer, would have been to discourage people from using the road, thereby lowering the number of casualties

Although the behaviour of all road user groups are likely to be influenced by the weather, by the very nature of how exposed to the elements pedestrians, pedal cyclists and motorcyclists are, these groups are likely to be more sensitive to abnormal weather.

Reported Road Casualties in Great Britain: Main Results in 2012


As the number of fatalities has been falling even during periods when both GDP and traffic were growing, the relationship between the economy and road accident fatalities is neither simple nor linear. What can be concluded, though, is that although there are other reasons for the large drop in fatalities over the last forty years, economic recessions have seemingly accelerated decreases in road accident deaths.
